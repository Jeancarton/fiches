That was the first time I had ever seen the planet hanging in space like that.
One of the astronauts said: “when we originally went to the Moon, our total focus was on the Moon. We weren’t thinking about looking back at the Earth, but now we’ve done it, that may well have been the most important reason we went.”
It’s this dynamic, alive place that you see glowing all the time.
It was pretty obvious from those descriptions we are stardust…
(The astronauts) were at some very deep level… realizing their interconnectedness with that beautiful blue green ball. It looks like a living breathing organism, but it also at the same time looks extremely fragile…

As you go into your mind in a contemplative way, the sense of the living reality of the planet becomes obvious, you become more in tune with the natural world.

A part of that is to come up with a new story, a new picture, a new way to approach this and to shift our behaviors in such a way that it leads to a sustainable approach to our civilization as opposed to a destructive approach.
We’re basically all living in this one ecosystem called Earth and everything that you do on one side of the ecosystem affects the other side and that is a new way for living for most of humanity.
It’s the oasis against the backdrop of infinity, this just enormous universe behind it. It’s placed perfectly from the Sun to take care of us.

In our obscurity, in all this vastness, there is no hint that help will come from elsewhere to save us from ourselves. The Earth is the only world known so far to harbor life. There is nowhere else, at least in the near future, to which our species could migrate. What is Nature ?

For decades people have unquestioningly accepted the idea that we should preserve Nature in its pre-human state. The “Anthropocene” is a term that some geologists are suggesting we should give to our current epoch, given how pervasive human influence has been over it.
25 years ago, environmental writer Bill McKibben said that climate change meant that every centimeter of the Earth was altered by man, then Nature was over. I think that Nature is anywhere where life thrives.

The environment

The natural environment encompasses all living and non-living things occurring naturally on Earth.
The environment represents the whole ecosystem that a society depends upon for various services such as water, materials, food and energy. When we refer to the environment, we are really talking about a combination of natural ecosystem and human economy.

Environmentalism

Environmentalism is a philosophy, ideology and social movement that seeks to improve and protect the quality of the natural environment through changes to environmentally harmful human activities, and through a reassessment of humanity's relationship with nature.
Environmentalism advocates the preservation, restoration and improvement of the natural environment and critical earth system elements or processes such as the climate. It may be referred to as a movement to control pollution or protect plant and animal diversity.
It is an attempt to balance relations between humans and the various natural systems on which they depend.

The origins of environmentalism

The earliest ideas of environment protectionism can be traced in 6th century BC in ancient India, with the symbiosis between all living beings and the five elements : earth, water, air fire, and space.
Arabic medical treaties were written during the 9th to 13th centuries dealing with environment.

Early environmental legislation in Britain

In British industrial cities, local reformers, especially after 1890, took the lead in identifying environmental degradation and pollution, and initiating grass-roots movements to demand reforms. It was only under the impetus of the Great Smog of 1952 in London, that the 1956 Clean Air Act was passed. It caused a rethinking of the dangers of environmental degradation to people’s quality of life.

The first environmental movements

An early “Back-to-Nature” movement, which anticipated the romantic ideal of modern environmentalism, was advocated by intellectuals such as John Ruskin, William Morris and George Bernard Shaw. The movement was a reaction to the urban conditions of the industrial towns.
Systematic efforts on behalf of the environment only began in the late 19th century.

The movement in the United States

It began in the late 19th century, out of concerns for protecting the natural resources of the West, with individuals such as John Muir and Henry David Thoreau making key philosophical contributions. The conservationist principles as well as the belief in an inherent right of Nature were to become the bedrock of modern environmentalism.

In the 20th century

In 1916, the National Park Service was founded by US president Woodrow Wilson.
The Sierra Club actions galvanized public opposition to building dams in the Grand Canyon and protected many other national treasures.

Post-war expansion : the sixties and the seventies

In 1962, Silent Spring by American biologist Rachel Carson, catalogued the environmental impacts of releasing large amounts of chemicals into the environment without fully understanding their effects on human health and ecology.
The book’s legacy was to produce a far greater awareness of environmental issues and interest into how people affect the environment. New pressure groups formed, notably Greenpeace and Friends of the Earth (US).
Protection of the environment also became important in the developing world, as in India under the influence of Mahatma Gandhi: peaceful methods of protest against deforestation (literally hugging trees, leading to the term “tree huggers”) were very influential.
Another milestone in the movement was the creation of Earth Day in San Francisco and other cities on 21st March 1970.

21st century and beyond

Enviromentalism continues to evolve to face up to new issues such as global warming, overpopulation, genetic engineering, and plastic pollution.

Ecology

Ecology addresses the full scale of life, from tiny bacteria to processes that span the entire planet. It is the study of how organisms interact with each other and their environment. It has a focus on biodiversity : an important focus for ecologists is to improve the understanding of how biodiversity affects ecological function. It is not synonymous with environmentalism. Ecologists seek to explain life processes.

Ecosystems

In 1964, Eugene Odum, called “the father of modern ecology”, insisted that “the whole is greater than the sum of its parts”; and that therefore reductionist scientific methods cannot adequately explain living systems.
Within the ecosystem, species are connected. Ecosystems are dynamically interacting systems of organisms. They are always changing.
An ecosystem’s area can vary greatly, from tiny to vast.

Life

Generally defined as areas that have not been significantly modified by human activity. Ecologists consider wilderness areas to be an integral part of th Earth's self-sustaining natural ecosystem.
Life : scientists generally accept that the biological manifestation of life is characterized by organization, metabolism, growth, adaptation, response to stimuli and reproduction.
According to the French thinker Edgar Morin, a living organization can only be understood as a function of a continual process of disorganization which degrades molecules and cells uninterruptedly as they are being reproduced.

The biosphere

Part of the Earth's outer shell within which life occurs. It is the global ecological system integrating all living beings and their relationships, including their interaction with the elements of the lithosphere, hydrosphere and atmosphere.
Over nine-tenths of the total biomass on Earth is plant life.

Ecological resilience

Capacity of a systems to absord disturbance and reorganize while undergoing change so as to still retain essentially the same function, identity, and feedbacks ; and it can also be the time required for an ecosystem to return to an equilibrium or steady-state following a perturbation. Human activities can adversely affect ecosystem resilience (such as deforestation, overfishing, pollution). Disturbances of sufficient magnitude can profoundly affect an ecosystem.

Economic resilience

Inherent to the successful operation of a free market is specialization which is required to achieve efficiency and increase productivity. This very act of specialization weakens resilience. This dependency reduces the ability of the system to adapt to these changes.

Human impacts on resilience : the example of agriculture

Feeding more than 7.8 billion human bodies takes a heavy toll on the Earth’s resources.
As a result of agricultural intensification, plant biodiversity is reduced.
This leads to a reduction in soil fertility and productivity. Interdisciplinary discourse on resilience now includes consideration of the interactions of humans and ecosystems via socio-ecological systems, and the need for shift from the maximum sustainable yield paradigm to environmental resource management.

Sustainability

Sustainable development is development that meets the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs. Four pillars of sustainability : economic, ecological, political and cultural sustainability. Sustainability implies responsible and proactive decision-making and innovation that minimize negative impacts.

Western industrial revolution tapped into the vast growth potential of the energy in fossil fuels. In the 21st century, there is increasing global awareness of the threat posed by the greenhouse effect.
Sustainability is studied and managed over many scales of time and space and in many contexts of environmental, social and economic organization. It can entail the full compass of biological and human activity or any part of it.
The underlying driver of direct human impacts on the environment is human consumption. This impact is reduced not only by consuming less but also by making the full cycle of production, use and disposal more sustainable.

The impact of consumerism

Ecological footprint

The ecological footprint measures human consumption in terms of the biologically productive land needed to provide the resources, and absorb the wastes of the average global citizen. The challenge is for countries to maintain an acceptable standard of living for their citizens while, at the same time, maintaining sustainable resource use. It is the combination of population increase in the developing world and unsustainable consumption levels in the developed world that poses a stark challenge to sustainability.

Overshoot day

Earth Overshoot Day marks the date we (all of humanity) have used more from Nature than our planet can renew in the entire year.

Sustainability economics

Historically there has been a close correlation between economic growth and environmental degradation: as communities grow, so the environment declines.
Sustainability economics represents : "ecological economics where environmental, ecological, social, cultural, health-related and monetary/financial aspects have to be integrated into the analysis". Much broader than the concepts of sustained yield of welfare, resources, or profit margins.
Consequently, long-term sustainability requires the transition to a steady state economy in which total GDP remains more or less constant.
Green economy : improves human well-being and social equity, while significantly reducing environmental risks and ecological scarcities. It takes greater account of the social and environmental consequences of market behavior.

The Neolithic Revolution
The shift from hunting and gathering to agriculture led to permanent settlements, the establishment of social classes, the eventual rise of urban living and large civilizations. Before the modern era, people were very much aware of their environmental, physical and biological limitations: the amount of land that each family could cultivate was limited by the large amount of human or animal labor that was necessary for agriculture.
The Modern Era
With a shift from a dominant pre-modern religious vision of the world to a modern scientific view came a whole new way of seeing the natural environment.
The Industrial Revolution
The Industrial Revolution represented a radical dislocation between society and the ecosystem. Farm sizes and worker productivity increased dramatically. The mass of society moved from working the land into the newly industrializing urban centers. A major part of this changing dynamic was due to the harnessing of powerful new energy sources. Coal, oil and gas enabled the transition to new mass manufacturing processes as they shifted from manual to mechanical.
Extraordinary exponential growth of human economic activity that began in the mid-20th century. All indicators for economy and ecosystem started changing at an exponential rate (from population growth to loss of species to energy consumption).
The AnthropoceneHuman industrial activity has become the primary driver of changes within earth systems. Unprecedented scale of alteration to Earth’s core systems such as overall biodiversity, climate or ocean acidity. Because of human intervention in the biosphere, it can no longer stabilize itself within the same equilibrium that has benefited societies in the past. The global economy is now the primary driver of change within ecosystems.
Sustainability
Sustainability then defines the ability of a system or process to endure over a period of time. How sustainable something is can be understood in terms of its overall efficiency in terms of how effective the whole organization is at operating within its environment. Sustainability is not a property of a thing. Sustainability is more what we call an emergent feature of whole systems. It is not so much about the parts as how the parts work together to enable effective overall outcomes.
An electric car is not really sustainable if the power system it is running on imports coal.
It is precisely because sustainability is about a relationship between a whole system and its environment that it cannot be achieved through optimizing individual parts. Sustainability is a complex and holistic challenge. It is the value and integrity of the whole organization that is required to maintain sustainability over time.

Nature as an economic externality

As ecosystem services are generally treated as economic externalities, they are unpriced and therefore overused and degraded.
The challenge of applying the concept of ecological resilience to the context of sustainable development is that it sits at odds with conventional economic ideology and policy making. The specialization that is required to achieve efficiency and increase productivity weakens resilience.
The economic importance of Nature is indicated by the use of the expression “ecosystem services” to highlight the market relevance of an increasingly scarce natural world that can no longer be regarded as both unlimited and free.

Gaia

In 1979, James Lovelock, a British scientist, published Gaia: A new look at life on Earth, which put forth the Gaia hypothesis; it proposes that life on earth can be understood as a single organism. The Gaia hypothesis proposes that the Earth is a self-regulating complex system.
That system as a whole, called Gaia, seeks a physical and chemical environment optimal for contemporary life.
The idea of the Earth as an integrated whole, a living being, has a long tradition. The mythical Gaia was the primal Greek goddess personifying the Earth.

Ecopsychology

Ecopsychology seeks to heal the alienation between the person and the natural environment. Denying the relevance of nature to our deepest emotional needs is still the rule in our culture generally. In devastating the environment we are undermining a basic requirement of sanity.

The inherent rights of Nature

The traditional environmental regulatory systems regard Nature as property to be used for human benefit.
Rights of Nature is grounded in the recognition that humankind and Nature share a fundamental, non-anthropocentric relationship given our shared existence on this planet.

Walden

1. Introduction

When I wrote the following pages, or rather the bulk of them, I lived alone, in the woods, a mile from any neighbour, in a house which I had built myself, on the shore of Walden pond, in COncord, Massachusetts, and earned my living by the labor of my hands only. I lived there two years and two months. At present I am a sojourner in civilized life again...
It is not merely a record of two years in the woods, but it also includes a reflection on that experience.

2. An indictment of society

Thoreau clearly aims at reforming the way of life of his neighbors in Concord, for as he sees it, they live as they were "doing penance". His tone is that of a moralist, a reformer.
Thoreau highlights a paradox: heirs to farms, contrary to appearances, are not privileged, but "misfortuned". Indeed, they are far from being advantaged, property deprives them of their freedom.
Instead of living rich spiritual lives, people waste away their strength amassing wealth.

The protestant work ethic held the belief that hard work was a way of honoring God and led to prosperous reward. Thoreau does not totally reject the Puritan virtues of thrift and industry, but he warns that, in a society obsessed with material values, people have lost sight of the initial principles.
They do not live "deliberately" but like machines, mindlessly, mechanically: "(Man) has no time to be anything but a machine".

The mass of men lead lives of quiet desperation. Most people do not complain: on the contrary, they keep "quiet" and try to pretend that everything is fine, concealing their distress because they are ashamed of it. Despair and the inability to act are grounded in the perception that we are powerless.

3. The cabin

The morning wind forever blows, the poem of creation is uninterrupted; but few are the ears that hear it.
Every morning was a cheerful invitation to make my life of equal simplicity.
The cabin is open to all winds, comfort is not the narrator's priority: whereas builders usually aim to achieve the best insulation available, in contrast, he rejoices at the fact that the cabin is open to the elements.
Man is invited to partake in the process of divine creation which unravels continuously as life goes on but, sadly, "few are the ears that hear it."

The cabin is no obstacle to the narrator's on-going dialogue with the elements.
Thoreau jubilates at the fact that his hut allows him to become "suddenly neighbors to the birds". Birds symbolize eternal life and freedom from constraints.
Thoreau suggests that the birds act as intermediaries between man's spiritual life and a more intellectual experience of reality.
Thoreau thus experiments a radically different kind of awareness of himself and of the world. Through his never-ending quest for purity, his constant striving for self-improvement, he has made a pact with Nature which requires a daily renewal, symbolized by his daily bath in the lake.

We must learn to reawaken and keep ourselves awake (...) by an infinite expectation of the dawn (...) I know of no more encouraging fact than the unquestionable ability of man to elevate his life by a conscious endeavor.
Through communion with Nature, everyone is called upon to create, not just material goods nor even external works of art. The utmost purpose is to transform one's outlook on life, one's relationship to the world so as to transfigure one's own life.

4. In praise of frugality

I thought often and seriously of picking huckleberries; that surely I could do, and its small profits might suffice, - for my greatest skill has been to want but little-
Most of the luxuries, and many of the so-called comforts of life, are not only not indispendable, but positive hindrances to the elevation of mankind.
Luxuries are positive obstacles to the elevation of human beings, meaning that they positively (that is to say "without a doubt") interfere with spiritual progress.

Men starve, not for want of necessaries, but for want of luxuries.

Once their physical needs are satisfied, people can and must achieve spiritual fulfillment, striking a balance that allows them to become truly human.

5. The lake

Even though, as vividly demonstrated in the case of Walden Pond, Nature is not treated with due reverence on the part of man, its resilience enables it to withstand use, misuse, abuse, even sacrilege.
Walden is inherently neither land, nor sky, but somethinf in-between.
It is continually receiving new life and motion from above. It is intermediate in its nature between land and sky.

The lake, which represents the essence of Nature, is personified. The gentle pulsing of its life, the heaving of its breast.

Unconsciously, even the humans embarking on a dehumanizing journey do not fail to absorb the beauty and peacefulness of the landscape and to receive its miraculous, beneficient and civilizing influence in the brief time they can catch sight of it. The sight of Walden is like a blessing which redeems even the non-believers.

6. How humans mistreat nature

Nature has no human inhabitants who appreciate her.
The villagers, who scarcely know where (Walden) lies, instead of going to the pond to bathe or drink, are thinking to bring its water, which should be as sacred as the Ganges at least, to the village in a pipe, to wash their dishes with! To earn their Walden by the turning of a cock or drawing of a plug.
Deep Cut is a stretch of railroad between Walden and Concord.
Flint's Pond! Such is the poverty of our nomenclature. What right had the unclean and stupid farmer, whose farm abutted on this sky water, whose shores he has ruthlessly laid bare, to give his name to it?
A rousseauist indictment of the human's mistreatment of Nature. Man's willful or unconscious, but persistent, attempts to defile his environment is most vividly exhibited in this passage.
In Thoreau's eyes, this symbolic gesture is akin to profanation because the water of the pond is of celestial, heavenly nature: "the unclean and stupid farmer" had no right to name the lake after himself just because his property bordered on it.
This condemnation of Flint's exacerbated and abusive sense of property conjures up another diatribe, written a century earlier. In his Discourse on the Origin and Basis of Inequality Among Men, published in 1754, the French philosopher Jean-Jacques Rousseau denounces The first man who, having fenced in a piece of land, said "this is mine", and found people naive enough to believe him, that man was the true founder of civil society... Beware of listening to this impostor; you are undone if you once forget that the fruits of the earth belong to us all, and the earth itself to nobody.
Far from protecting Nature, the law also allows farmers to exhaust their land and even the waters if they have a mind to it.
Flint would trade God for money, he worships money over the most sacred things.
Nature has no human inhabitants who appreciate her.
White Pond and Walden : How much more beautiful than our lives, how much more transparent than our characters, are they! (...) We never learned meanness of them.

7. Nurturing Nature

Thoreau expresses a longing for an Edenic time, before the appearance of agriculture and permanent settlements, when humans lived in the midst of Nature as hunters and gatherers.
Estranged from Nature, modern humans need the tonic of wildness.
170 years ago, Thoreau pleaded in favor of a closer link between humans and Nature. The XXIst century reader is fully aware that humans need to be in touch with Nature for their physical and psychological well-being.
The cycles of life, the interdependance of the species, fierce competition and the fight for survival as well as natural selection make Nature a dangerous, ruthless environment whose laws are beyond the humans' control.

The communion with Nature experienced by Thoreau has a soothing effect on humans: The most sweet and tender, the most innocent and encouraging society may be found in any natural object.
While I enjoy the friendship of the seasons, I trust that nothin can make life a burden to me.

8. In praise of idleness

The author marvels at the intensity of his life, at the wealth of sensations and emotions that he enjoys in the midst of nature.
I was rich, if not in money, in sunny hours and summer days.
Paradoxically, the words "stolen, spend, valued, rich, money, lavishly, waste" are used to refer to the invaluable, immaterial benefits of his lifestyle.
Thoreau's philosophy is in constrast with the protestant work ethic that prevailed in his society, emphasizing diligence and hard work as a way to obtain material success, please God and attain salvation.

9. Thoreau's pledge

I wished to live deliberately. "Deliberately" means "with careful consideration, thoughtfully, leisurely and steadily", as opposed to "impulsively".
Whether or not it is necessary "to practise resignation" is not something Thoreau takes for granted. Like the Stoics, Thoreau considers that it is right to resign only when it is absolutely necessary and only to what is beyond our control.

Thoreau claims total freedom of expression, in form as well as in substance. He wants to impart to his readers the intuitive knowledge which he has gained "beyond the narrow limits of (his) daily experience".

For Thoreau, mindless submission to the norms in the name of common sense is equivalent to going through life deeply asleep.

I cannot count one. I know not the first letter of the alphabet.
He opposes knowledge and wisdom as mutually exclusive : I have always been regretting that I was not as wise as the day I was born.

10. Know thyself

Thoreau invites us to explore ourselves, our "own higher latitudes". "Every man is the lord of a realm", the realm being no other than himself: each of us is by right of birth a prince, heir to an inner kingdom.
What Thoreau explains here is that we have to accept to lose our bearings, that is abandon our false certitudes.

11. Becoming yourself

If a man does not keep pace with his companions, perhaps it is because he hears a different drummer. Let him step to the music which he hears.
Thoreau denounces our "desperate haste to succeed". Hopeless and dangerous, because those who are in a hurry to succeed are doomed to fail because they do not take the time to reflect on their actions.
If one advances confidently in the direction of his dreams, and endeavors to live the life which he has imagined, he will meet with a success unexpected in common hours.
Thoreau points out that what matters is achieving personal, subjective happiness.
